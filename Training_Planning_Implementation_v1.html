1. Training Coordinator ▾
Definition
  • An individual within an organization responsible for overseeing various aspects of the training process.
  • Also known as Training Program Coordinator.
Role of Training Coordinator
  • Create training schedules for all company departments; track, create reports on outcomes of all training; maintain training records.
  • Train new hires on company policies and procedures; use the best training methods for a specific purpose or audience.
  • Gather and evaluate information from employees and management on previous training to identify weaknesses and areas needing additional training.
  • Attend seminars and meetings to learn new training methods and techniques; use knowledge to prepare and coordinate future training sessions.
  • Recruit and train new trainers; delegate training tasks; evaluate performance.
  • Inform employees on scheduled training and track their progress.
  • Recommend training materials and methods; order and maintain in-house training equipment and facilities; manage the budget set for training.
Skills and Qualifications Required
  • Excellent time management skills, public speaking skills, problem-solving skills, and both verbal and written communication skills.
  • Proficiency in word processing and presentation software.
  • Ability to operate media equipment such as projectors and personal computers.
  • Knowledge about traditional and modern training methods and techniques.
  • Exceptional organization skills, leadership, and interpersonal skills.
  • Ability to work with a team and have attention to detail.
  • Knowledgeable about learning management systems, instructional design, and e-learning platforms.
  • Ability to handle multiple assignments and assess and analyze data.
Training Coordinator vs Human Resources Coordinator

Training Coordinator

  • Specializes in creating, implementing, and reviewing training initiatives
  • Narrow focus — only on training

Human Resources Coordinator

  • Much wider area of job focus across various Human Resources department duties
  • Handles recruitment, onboarding, records, benefits, complaints

2. Training – Meaning, Types and Components ▾
What is Training?
  • An organization's efforts to improve an individual's ability to perform a job or organizational role.
  • An organized procedure by which people learn knowledge and / or skills for a definite purpose.
  • Concerned with providing an individual with the opportunity to learn what he or she needs in order to do their job more effectively.
  • The art of doing the job in a correct, effective, and efficient manner.
💡
Key Summary Points Training is a systematic learning process · a communication process · results in changes in knowledge, skill and practice · improves employees' productivity · helps achieve organizational goals. Training and education are not the same.
Philosophies of Training
  • Organizations should have a clear long-term view of the knowledge, attitude, and skill levels of competencies that employees need.
  • Training should be relevant and based on need — not stereotyped and static.
  • Training should be problem-based with a mix of theory and practical application.
  • It should be action-oriented.
  • Training should be related to the performance of an employee.
  • Training should be a continuous process — not a one-time affair.
Types of Training — Based on Structure
  • Structured Training: Structure is designed by the training institution; planned well in advance; covered within stipulated time period by expert group meetings.
  • Semi-Structured Training: Curriculum can be changed to suit trainee needs during the programme; trainee reacts only when training gets momentum. Example: Classroom Training (also known as Instructor-Led Training). Provides structured learning environment; face-to-face instruction; real-time interaction; peer collaboration.
  • Unstructured Training: Trainees themselves structure the programme with the trainer; objectives must be clear to both trainer and trainee; only suitable for manageable group size.
Types of Training — Based on Context
  • Orientation / Induction Training: Provides general information about organizational structure, service rules, code of conduct, facilities. Objective: prepare the employee to perform their job effectively.
  • Refresher Training: Conducted periodically to keep employees updated in professional knowledge and skills. Helps organizations increase productivity and improve quality of output.
Other Types of Training

Direct Contact Training

  • Trainers and learners work one-on-one
  • Limits number of participants
  • On campus or classroom training

Distance Training

  • Responds to needs of large numbers of trainees
  • Reaches participants wherever they are
  • Learners learn at their own pace

Formal Training

  • Classroom situation with strict structured time and curriculum schedule
  • Most visible and recognized in labour market

Non-Formal Training

  • Alternative to formal programmes
  • Avoids rigidities of classroom
  • Conducted in open environment

Centralized Training

  • One institute formally charged with planning, staffing, funding, ensuring conformity to standards
  • Predominates in developing countries

Dispersed Training

  • Learning activities spread over time and location
  • Greater flexibility; adaptable to individual schedules
Components of Training
Goal Motivation Opportunity to Learn Opportunity to Practice Follow Up Retraining
Generations of Distance Education
  • First Generation — Correspondence Model: Print-based learning.
  • Second Generation — Multimedia Model: Print, Audio tapes, Video tapes.
  • Third Generation — Telelearning Model: Audio and teleconferencing, videoconferencing, broadcasting.
  • Fourth Generation — Flexible Learning Model: Interactive multimedia, Computer-mediated communication (communication between two or more persons via separate computers through the Internet or network using computer software).

3. Linkage Between Institute and Industry — DST Scheme ▾
Importance of Linkage
  • Widely recognized as an essential requirement to train and develop the right kind of technical manpower to sustain and promote industrial growth.
  • Schemes introduced: Practice School Programmes, Sandwich Programmes, and Cooperative Programmes.
  • Linkages with industries are mainly developed for: Providing On the Job Training and absorbing in wage and self-employment.
  • Linkage should be viewed as total involvement in: Utilization of facilities in industries, utilization of technicians for skill training, evaluation and placement of students.
Factors Inhibiting Industry-Institute Linkage
Curriculum Teachers Contact With Industry Innovation and Developmental Activities Leadership
Dual System of Training (DST) Scheme
  • Introduced in 2016 to enable industries and establishments to partner with Government and Private Industrial Training Institutes for conducting training programmes.
  • Amalgamation of theoretical training (through ITIs) and practical training (through industry).
  • Revised guidelines launched by Director General of Training in February 2019; effective from academic year 2019.
  • Under DST, On the Job Training is conducted in the industry environment while theoretical component is covered in the Industrial Training Institute itself.
  • Students appear in regular examination under Craftsman Training Scheme and awarded e-certificate.
  • Applicable in all affiliated Industrial Training Institutes; covers all existing trades under Craftsman Training Scheme.
  • Trainees awarded E-National Trade Certificate after completion — have an edge over regular Industrial Training Institute pass outs in terms of employability.

What is Curriculum?
  • Derived from the Latin word "currere" meaning "run" or "runway" or a running course.
  • Thus, curriculum means a course to be run for reaching a certain goal.

Problem-Based Learning (PBL)
  • A pedagogical approach enabling students to learn while engaging actively with meaningful problems.
  • Students solve problems in a collaborative setting; create mental models; form self-directed learning habits through practice and reflection.
  • Does not focus on problem solving with a defined solution — allows development of knowledge acquisition, enhanced group collaboration, and communication.
Constructivism
  • Based on the idea that people actively construct their own knowledge; reality is determined by experiences as a learner.
  • Learners use previous knowledge as a foundation and build on it with new things they learn.
  • Principles: Learner is not passive but an active cognizing subject. Cognition organizes the learner's experiences of the world — not merely discovers or represents it.
Flipped Classroom
  • An instructional strategy and type of blended learning aiming to increase student engagement by having pupils complete readings at home and work on live problem-solving during class time.
  • Students watch online lectures, collaborate in online discussions, or carry out research at home; engage concepts in the classroom with a mentor's guidance.

5. Development of Media for Instructions ▾
What is Instructional Media?
  • Tools, materials, and resources used to facilitate teaching and learning processes in educational settings.
  • Designed to enhance instruction, engage learners, and convey information effectively.
  • Written Instructional Material (WIM) is one type of instructional media.

Individualized Instructional Media
📖
Programmed Learning Material Presents learning material in small steps called frames. Each frame consists of information plus a question requiring active and overt response. 95% of responses are designed to be correct. Immediate feedback and reinforcement is provided.
💻
Computer-Assisted Instruction (CAI) Based on interaction between computer and learner. Presents verbal and visual information with sound in multimedia. Uses computer capabilities to simulate, animate, replicate, generate new examples.
Instructional Modes in Computer-Assisted Instruction
  • Drill and Practice Mode: Computer presents series of exercises; provides congratulatory or corrective feedback; learner controls the pace.
  • Tutorial Mode: Information presented in small bits followed by questions; computer analyzes learner's response and provides appropriate feedback.
  • Simulation Mode: Learning experiences regarding real-life systems. Example: Gaseous Laws, Nitrogen Cycle, Ecological system.
  • Discovery Mode: Uses inductive approach — problems are presented and learner solves through trial and error.
  • Gaming Mode: Teaching imparted through play or game.
Inductive vs Deductive Approach to Learning

Inductive Approach

  • Present specific examples first
  • Learners derive general principles from examples
  • Bottom-up reasoning

Deductive Approach

  • Present general principles first
  • Then apply to specific instances
  • Top-down reasoning
Augmented Reality in Training
  • Blending of digital elements with real-world environment in real time.
  • Superimposes graphics, audio, and other sensory enhancements over a real-world environment.
  • Examples: Pokemon Go, Matrix movie, Iron Man (movie), Mission Impossible.
  • United States Army uses Augmented Reality tools to create digitally enhanced training missions for soldiers.
  • Sixth Sense Technology by Pranav Mistry (MIT Labs) is an example of Augmented Reality.
  • India's first Augmented Reality education and training institute to be set up in Varanasi in partnership with Eon Reality (United States-based company).
Learning Management System (LMS)
  • A software application or web-based technology used to plan, implement, and assess a specific learning process.
  • Delivers and manages all types of content including videos, courses, workshops, and documents.
  • Consists of two elements: a server (base functionality) and a user interface operated by instructors, students, and administrators.
Swayam Prabha
  • Initiative of the Ministry of Education.
  • A group of 40 Direct-to-Home channels devoted to telecasting high-quality educational programmes on 24 by 7 basis using the GSAT-15 satellite.
  • Covers Higher Education and School Education.

8. Entrepreneurship Development ▾
Key Definitions
  • Entrepreneur: An innovative person who maximizes profits by following new strategies or venturing into new products/services. Derived from the French word "entreprendre" meaning "to undertake".
  • Enterprise: The entity or business initiated by an entrepreneur.
  • Entrepreneurship: The process of the entrepreneur — an attempt to create value through recognition of business opportunity using communicative and management functions to mobilize financial and material resources.
📌
SVO AnalogyEntrepreneur (Subject / The Person / The Actor) → Entrepreneurship (Verb / The Process / The Act) → Enterprise (Object / The Outcome)
Schumpeter's Four Forms of Entrepreneur
The Achiever The Sales Person The Manager The Inventor
Types of Entrepreneurship
  • Opportunity-Based: Career chosen based on available opportunity and acquired knowledge. Real entrepreneurship — idea initiated from within. Entrepreneur is innovative, enthusiastic, and creative.
  • Necessity-Based: Career chosen as a last chance due to family circumstances or job disappointment. Not of choice and interest but of pressure and compulsion.
  • Technological: Entrepreneur introduces new technology to the market. Example: Using solar drier for drying fish — the new process makes it technological.
  • Geographical: Entrepreneur distributes technology or product from one place to another at national or international level. Major challenge: analyzing consumer demand. Example: Canned fish products made in Kerala sold in Uttar Pradesh.
Classification of Entrepreneurs — Based on Expertise
  • Technical Entrepreneurs: Develop improved quality of goods through craftsmanship. Greatest strength is skill in production technique.
  • Non-Technical Entrepreneurs: Not concerned with technical aspects. Focus more on marketing and distribution.
  • Professional Entrepreneurs: Focus on establishing a business, not managing it. Sell running business and start another venture.
Classification — Based on Motivation Levels
  • Pure Entrepreneurs: Motivated by psychological and economic rewards; personal satisfaction, ego, or status.
  • Induced Entrepreneurs: Motivated by government policy measures — assistance and concessions to start a venture.
  • Motivated Entrepreneurs: Influenced by desire for self-fulfillment; driven by possibility of making and marketing a new product.
  • Spontaneous Entrepreneurs: Start business by natural talents. Self-motivation drives entrepreneurial activity.
Classification — Based on Type of Business
  • Business Entrepreneurs: Conceive idea for new product or service; utilize production and marketing resources.
  • Trading Entrepreneurs: Undertake trading activities, not manufacturing; identify potential markets; stimulate demand.
  • Industrial Entrepreneurs: Essentially manufacturers who identify customer needs and tailor products or services.
  • Corporate Entrepreneurs: Demonstrate skill in organizing and managing corporate undertaking; follow corporate regulations.
  • Agricultural Entrepreneurs: Undertake agricultural activities like raising and marketing crops, fertilizers, and agriculture inputs.

Essential Qualities of Entrepreneurs
  • Strong Desire: To achieve a higher goal and make dreams come true.
  • Consistency: Consistent in decision and follow-up; not deterred by difficulties.
  • Risk Taking: Take moderate risk rather than wild speculative gamble. (Note: Entrepreneurs are moderate risk takers.)
  • Opportunities Analysis: Quick to see and grab opportunities; innovative turn of mind; convert difficulties into opportunities.
  • Feedback: Take immediate feedback on performance; seek prompt and accurate data even if unfavorable.
  • Planning: Set goals and make systematic plans to achieve them within a certain time limit.
Three I's of Entrepreneurial Skills

Initiative

  • Ability to initiate things in correct time and efficient manner
  • Primary skill for an entrepreneur

Independent

  • Face all problems and solve strategically
  • Take decisions independently even while consulting others

Ideas

  • Efficient enterprise success depends on new ideas
  • Ideas must be backed by creative thinking to make product unique and popular
Six Cs that Motivate Entrepreneurs

Intrapreneurship
  • A system allowing an employee to act like an entrepreneur within a company or organization.
  • Intrapreneurs are self-motivated, proactive, and action-oriented.
  • An intrapreneur knows failure does not have a personal cost — the organization absorbs losses arising from failure.
  • Often takes the form of a semi-autonomous group (internal venture team), operating within the overarching structure of the parent organization.

9. Safety at Workplace ▾
Industrial Safety
  • Encompasses safety management practices in the industrial sector to protect industrial workers, machinery, facilities, structures, and the environment.
  • Involves comprehensive management of all operations and procedures to safeguard employees and assets by minimizing hazards, risks, and accidents.
Types of Industrial Incidents
  • Near Misses: No damage or injury, but a slight variation could have led to harm.
  • Minor Incidents: Involving small-scale damage or injury.
  • Serious Incidents: Causing significant damage and injury.
  • Fatal Incidents: Resulting in loss of life and potentially severe property damage.
Causes of Industrial Incidents
Human Causes Mechanical Causes Environmental Causes
Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
  • Protective clothing, helmets, goggles, or other garments designed to protect the wearer's body from injury or infection.
  • Hazards addressed: physical, electrical, heat, chemical, biohazards, and airborne particulate matter.
  • Purpose: reduce employee exposure to hazards when engineering controls and administrative controls are not feasible or effective.
  • Types: Helmet (hazard: falling objects, spatter), Foot Protection (hot spatter, falling objects, working wet area), Respiratory Protective Equipment (dust particles, fumes/gases/vapours), Arms and Hand Protection (heat burn, blows spark, electric shock), Eyes and Face Protection (flying dust particles, ultraviolet rays, infrared rays).
Occupational Safety, Health, and Working Conditions Code 2020
  • Emphasizes health, safety, and welfare of workers across industry, trade, business, manufacturing, factory, transport, construction, newspaper establishments, audio-video production, plantation, mine, dock-work, and services.
  • Applicable to: factories with 20 or more workers with power aid; factories with 40 or more workers without power aid.
  • Does not apply to Central Government offices, State Government offices, or any warship.
  • Does apply to contract labour employed through contractors where Central or State Government is the principal employer.
  • Establishments must register within 60 days electronically.
  • Subsumed 13 Acts including Factories Act 1948, Mines Act 1952, Motor Transport Workers Act 1961, and others.
Safety Committees — Required When
  • Factory with 500 or more workers.
  • Factory carrying on hazardous process with 250 or more workers.
  • Building or construction work with 250 or more workers.
  • Mine with 100 or more workers.
National Safety Council (NSC)
  • Set up by Ministry of Labour, Government of India on 4 March 1966.
  • Apex not-for-profit making tripartite body; registered under Societies Registration Act 1860 and Bombay Public Trust Act 1950.
  • Activities: specialised training programmes, safety audits, risk assessment, Safety Health and Environment promotional materials, National Safety Day/Week, Fire Service Week, World Environment Day, Road Safety Week.

Classes of Fire

Fire Triangle and Fire Tetrahedron
  • Fire Triangle: Three essential elements — Oxygen, Heat, Fuel.
  • Fire Tetrahedron: Four factors — Oxygen, Heat, Fuel, Chemical Chain Reaction.
Fire Extinguishing Methods
  • Cooling: Removing heat. Cooling with water is the most popular method; heat generated by fire is absorbed by water.
  • Starving: Removing fuel source. When fire runs out of flammable materials, it burns out. Example: cutting gas supply immediately extinguishes gas fire.
  • Smothering: Cutting off oxygen supply. Without oxygen, fire burns out. Effective for commercial fires.

11. First Aid Techniques ▾
What is First Aid?
  • First and immediate assistance given to any person with either a minor or serious illness or injury.
  • Purpose: preserve life, prevent the condition from worsening, or promote recovery until medical services arrive.
Purpose of First Aid
Preserve life of victim Promote recovery Prevent further injury or damage Prevent complications Maintain comfort of victim Help victim reach medical care
Good Samaritan
  • A person who gives First Aid to a stranger in emergency.
  • Every first aid provider is a Good Samaritan.
  • Good Samaritan Laws: Laws that guide and protect the Good Samaritan. Every first aid provider has the duty to be careful and responsible in their actions while helping the victim.
Burns Classification
  • Superficial Burn: Only the outermost layer of skin (epidermis) is burned.
  • Partial Thickness Burn: Destroys the epidermis; generally very painful; fatal if wounds are on more than 20 per cent of the body.
  • Full Thickness Burn: Not painful in most cases (which may mislead the first-aider); needs immediate medical attention.
First Aid for Severe Burns — 6 Steps
1
Help the casualty lie downDo not allow injury area to come into direct contact with the ground.
2
Pour cold water for minimum 10 minutesSimultaneously arrange transport to hospital.
3
Gently remove jewellery and burnt clothingRemove ring, watch, belt, shoes before tissues begin to swell. Remove clothing only if not sticking to skin.
4
Cover with sterile dressingProtect injury from infection.
5

6

RICE Method — For Strains, Sprains, Contusions
R
Rest
Stop using injured part to prevent further injury, delay healing, or increase pain
I
Ice

C

Elevate injured part above heart level using objects and pillows. Decreases swelling and pain.

Types of Dressings
  • Gauze Dressings: Most commonly used; loosely woven cotton fabric; allows air circulation and absorption of wound exudate.
  • Adhesive Dressings (Band-Aids): Pre-made with adhesive backing; used for minor cuts, scrapes, and abrasions.
  • Hydrocolloid Dressings: Gel-forming substance; absorbs wound exudate; creates moist environment; waterproof; can be left for several days; useful for shallow wounds and pressure sores.
  • Foam Dressings: Soft absorbent foam; manages moderate to heavy wound exudate; used for ulcers, burns, and surgical wounds.
  • Alginate Dressings: Made from seaweed-derived fibers; highly absorbent; form gel-like consistency; ideal for heavily exuding wounds, leg ulcers, and cavity wounds.
  • Transparent Film Dressings: Thin flexible clear film; waterproof; allows easy monitoring without removing; suitable for minor abrasions, surgical incisions, and superficial burns.
Types of Bandages
  • Crepe Bandage (Gauze or Roller Bandage): Woven strip of absorbent material; versatile — used for holding dressings, medium injury support, compression.
  • Compression Bandages: Long strip of stretchable material; supports soft tissue injuries by applying pressure.
  • Snake Bite Bandage: Injury-specific compression bandage for bites and stings; has special indicator markings for correct tension.
  • Triangular Bandages: Multi-purpose right-angled triangle cloth; used as strap for injured limbs, securing dressings, compression for swelling or bleeding wounds.
  • Tubular Bandages: Elasticated gauze woven in continuous circle; provides uniform compression; used for arms, legs, ankles, and wrists.
  • Cohesive Bandages: Self-adhesive material; sticks to itself but not to skin or hair; no need for bandage clips.

12. Teacher Training Programmes ▾
Overview
  • Professional development of teachers is not an event — it is a continuous process.
  • In India, teacher training programmes are categorized as pre-service and in-service teacher training programmes.
Historical Milestones of In-Service Teacher Training
  • Secondary Education Commission (1952-53 — Mudaliyar Commission): Highlighted role of in-service teacher training. Training colleges should provide refresher courses, short intensive courses, practical training in workshops, seminars, and professional conferences.
  • Education Commission (1964-66): Recommended "school complexes" with a nodal school shouldering responsibility for continuous professional development of all teachers.
  • National Commission of Teachers-I (1983-85 — "Teacher and Society"): Every teacher must attend in-service training of 3 weeks duration once in a block of five years; linked with career promotion.
  • National Policy of Education 1986: "Teacher education is a continuous process, and its pre-service and in-service components are inseparable."
  • Acharya Ramamurthi Review Committee (1990): In-service and refresher courses should be related to specific needs of teachers; evaluation and follow-up should be part of the scheme.
  • National Knowledge Commission (2006-09): Commented that in-service training shows problems of inadequate quantity, uneven quality, outdated syllabi, and poor management. In-service courses need to be incentivized.
Continuous Professional Development (CPD)
  • Process by which teachers acquire, develop, and strengthen their skills and know-how to become more effective.
  • Ongoing process in response to the ever-changing professional environment.
  • Includes: updating knowledge, improving classroom practices, dealing with emerging challenges, professional networking using Information and Communication Technology-based networking.
Micro-Teaching
  • A teacher training technique involving breaking down the teaching process into smaller, manageable components for practice and reflection.
  • Concept introduced by Dr. Dwight W. Allen at Stanford University in the 1960s.
  • Provides opportunity for teachers to focus on specific teaching skills, receive feedback, and refine instructional techniques.
  • Mini-lesson typically lasts 10–15 minutes.
  • Components per Universal Grants Commission: A teacher, pupils (usually 5–7), a brief lesson, objectives of the specific micro-teaching occasion, feedback by supervisor or via audio/video tape recordings or closed circuit television.
Procedure of Micro-Teaching (9 Steps)
1
Defining a SkillParticular skill is defined to trainees in terms of teaching behaviors.
2
Demonstrating the SkillSpecific skill demonstrated by experts or shown through videotape or film.
3
Planning the LessonStudent teacher plans a short micro lesson with supervisor's help.
4
Teaching the LessonPupil teacher teaches lesson to small group (5–10). Observed by supervisor or recorded.
5
DiscussionTeaching is followed by discussion to provide feedback to the trainee.
6
Re-PlanningPupil teacher re-plans the lesson in light of discussion and suggestions.
7
Re-TeachingRevised lesson retaught to another small group of students.
8
Re-DiscussionRe-teaching followed by discussion and feedback again provided.
9
Repeating the CycleThe teach-reteach cycle is repeated till the desired level of skill is achieved.

13. New Trends in Training and Impact of Globalization and Industrialization ▾
New Trends in Training
  • Artificial Intelligence: Learning tools powered by Artificial Intelligence provide resources and training programmes specifically designed for each employee.
  • Gamification: Incorporation of game concepts into employee training programmes. Boosts motivation, participation, engagement, and productivity.
  • Personalized Training: Individualized employee training programmes customized to each employee's learning requirements; results in improved productivity outcomes.
  • Mentoring: Experienced individuals guide less experienced employees.
  • Need-Based Training: Training designed specifically around identified needs.
  • Micro-Teaching: Breaking training into smaller focused components (covered in detail above).
Impact of Globalization on Training
  • Led to more diverse workforces — training programmes now include cultural awareness and sensitivity components.
  • Drives need for standardization while also necessitating adaptation to local contexts.
  • Electronic learning platforms, Virtual Reality, and Augmented Reality now commonly used for remote and interactive training sessions.
  • Global market demands skills like digital literacy, language proficiency, and cross-cultural communication.
  • Greater focus on soft skills like adaptability and problem-solving.
  • Global operations require compliance with various international laws and regulations — training now includes modules on international standards.
  • Increased importance of continuous learning and lifelong professional development.
  • Led to outsourcing of training services to specialized firms across different regions.
Impact of Industrialization on Training
  • Shift from Craftsmanship to Factory-Based Skills: Pre-industrial era training was apprenticeship-based; Industrial era shifted to training workers for specific tasks within a production line — focus on efficiency and specialization.
  • Industrialization necessitated standardized training programmes to ensure consistency and quality in mass production.
  • Need for technical skills led to establishment of technical schools and vocational training centres.
  • As industries expanded, on-the-job training became essential.
  • Large industrial companies started their own training departments for employee development including safety protocols and management training.
  • With industrialization came greater awareness of worker safety and introduction of labour laws and regulations.
