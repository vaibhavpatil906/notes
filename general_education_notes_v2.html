Education: Meaning, Types & Social Change

Etymology • Formal/Informal/Non-formal • Social Mobility • Global Social Mobility Index

Etymology of "Education"
  • Educare (Latin) = "to bring up" or "rear"
  • Educere (Latin) = "to bring out" or "lead forth"
  • Education = process of rearing + bringing out potentialities of an individual
  • Broadest sense = deliberate and systematic attempt to socialize an individual
  • Knowledge acquired and passed on from one generation to another
Gandhi: "Education leads to all-round development." → Basic Education: schooling integrated with productive work → school as self-reliant productive institution.
Amartya Sen: Education as basic capability enhancing freedom → Capability Approach: holistic understanding of human development.
Three Types of Education
TypeDescription / Examples
FormalInstitutionalized (schools, colleges); standardized pre-decided curriculum; sequential levels
InformalFamily, relatives at home; unstructured, lifelong process
Non-formalFor out-of-school children; outside formal system. Ex: NGOs, adult literacy, vocational centres, skill dev courses
MPSC Rajyaseva 2019 – Formal Education features: (a) Classroom + direct teacher presence; (b) Pre-decided curriculum; (d) Sequential levels → next level needs prior level completion. ✅ Ans: (2) a, b, d correct
Education as Tool for Social Change
  • Empowering individuals
  • Increased Participation (civic, economic)
  • Improved economic status & Poverty Eradication
  • Upward Social Mobility
  • Better health and nutritional status
  • Personality development and character building
Social Mobility – Types
  • Social Mobility: shift in individual's social status from one to another
  • Horizontal Mobility: occupation changes but social standing unchanged (doctor → medical teacher)
  • Vertical Mobility: change in occupational/political/religious status → societal change
    • Upward = lower → higher | Downward = reverse
  • Inter-generational: social position changes across generations (father = factory worker; son = lawyer)
  • Intra-generational: change within single generation lifespan / between siblings
Global Social Mobility Index
Prepared by: World Economic Forum
First released: 2020
5 Determinants:
• Health
• Education (access, quality & equity, lifelong learning)
• Technology
• Work (opportunities, wages, conditions)
• Protection & Institutions
Education sub-factors:
Access • Quality & Equity • Lifelong Learning

Evolution of General Education in India

Colonial Era (1781–1944) → Key Acts, Commissions & Education Conferences

1781
Calcutta Madrasa – Warren Hastings
Governor-General of Bengal Warren Hastings established for Islamic law studies. First educational institute set by East India Company (EIC).
1784
Asiatic Society of Bengal – William Jones
Founded by William Jones to understand India's history and culture. Charles Wilkins translated Bhagwat Gita into English during this period.
1791
Sanskrit College, Benares – Jonathan Duncan
Resident of Benares Jonathan Duncan founded for study of Hindu laws and philosophies.
1800
Fort William College, Calcutta – Richard Wellesley
Governor-General Richard Wellesley to train EIC civil servants in Indian languages and customs.
1813
Charter Act of 1813
Set aside annual sum of Rs.1 lakh for educating Indian subjects.
1835
Macaulay's Minutes
• Resources for western sciences & literature in English ONLY
• English as medium of education in all schools/colleges
• Mass education neglected
• Downward Filtration Theory: educate small section of upper/middle-class Indians to bridge masses and government.
1854
Wood's Dispatch — "Magna Carta of English Education in India"
• Sacred duty of government to provide education
• Masses in mother tongue; English knowledge still required
• Grants-in-aid system for educational institutions
• First universities at Kolkata, Chennai, Mumbai

Key Features: Vernacular in primary schools • Anglo-vernacular high schools • Affiliated colleges at district level • Universities in presidency towns • Impetus to female education & vocational training • Secular education in govt schools
1868 & 1875
Lord Mayo's Colleges
Rajkot College in Kathiawar (1868) and Mayo College of Ajmer (1875) for political training of Indian princes and elites.
1882
Hunter's Commission (Report 1882)
• Careful withdrawal of govt from higher ed; private enterprise to take over
• State focus on primary education; moral side emphasized
Recommendations: Mass education via vernacular languages • Transfer primary control to district/municipal boards • Female education outside presidency towns • Secondary divided: Literary (→university) & Vocational (commercial)
1902
Raleigh Commission → Universities Act 1904
Viceroy Curzon believed universities produced revolutionary students. Commission led to Universities Act 1904: focus on research over revolutionary activities; govt veto on senate; stricter affiliation rules.
1910
Gopal Krishna Gokhale
Mooted idea of free and compulsory primary education for all children — plan shelved then but remains relevant.
1917–19
Sadler University Commission
Initially to study Calcutta University; ended up reviewing all universities.
• Stressed trained teachers • School in 12 years • University after intermediate (not matric) → 3-year degree • Separate board for secondary & intermediate
• 1916-21: 7 new universities at Mysore, Patna, Benares, Aligarh, Dacca, Lucknow, Osmania
1920
Central Advisory Board of Education (CABE)
Set up to advise government on education. 1937-39: stressed adult education on wider scale; involve voluntary organizations.
1937
Wardha Scheme of Basic Education
By INC. Committee under Zakir Hussain. Based on Gandhiji's ideas in Harijan (magazine, 1933).

Three Basic Resolutions (22-23 Oct 1937):
1. Free & Compulsory Education for 8 years
2. Mother Tongue as Medium of Instruction
3. Education around manual/productive work

Features: Age 6-14 (5 yrs Junior + 3 yrs Senior) • Gender/culture/religion-neutral • Girls may leave at 12 if parents wish • Work-centric (regional craftwork)
1944
Sergeant Plan of Education (by CABE)
State responsible for adult education.
• Free primary: age 3-6 yrs | Compulsory: age 6-11 yrs | High school for selected: age 11-17 yrs
• Focus: teachers' training, physical education, handicapped education
MPSC Chronological Order Trick: Macaulay (1835) → Wood's Dispatch (1854) → Hunter Commission (1882) → Sargent Report (1944) → Ans: (a) 2, 1, 4, 3

Post-Independence Education Commissions

Radhakrishnan • Mudaliar • Kothari • Ramamurthi • Janardhana Reddy

Radhakrishnan Commission (1948-49)
University Education CommissionFirst Commission of Independent India
  • First Education Commission of independent India
  • Quality of university education depends on quality of teachers
  • Improve pay scale & service conditions; leaves for higher studies
  • University admission: after intermediate (after 11 yrs school)
  • Essay type supplemented by objective type questions
  • Oral examinations should be used
  • All students should receive N.C.C. training
  • Establish colleges for women with adequate facilities
Mudaliar Commission (1952-53) – Secondary Education
Secondary Education Commission
  • Chairman: Dr. Lakshamanswami Mudaliar, VC of Madras University
  • Methods: Questionnaire Method & Interview Method
  • Secondary education: children aged 11-17 years
  • Divided into 2 parts:
    • Junior Secondary Stage (3 years)
    • Higher Secondary Stage (4 years)
Kothari Commission (1964-66) – Education Commission
First Comprehensive CommissionLed to NPE 1968
  • First comprehensive commission covering ALL levels (primary → higher)
  • Report: "Education and National Development"
  • Recommended 10+2+3 pattern — accepted mainly to prepare school leavers for vocations
  • 6% of national income for education
  • At least one agricultural university per state
  • Three Language Formula
✅ MPSC 2014: "Last commission to look into education system as a whole" → D.S. Kothari Commission
✅ MPSC 2022: Kothari report title → "Education and National Development"
Acharya Ramamurthi Committee (1990)
Review of NPE 1986
  • Formed 1989 under Acharya Ramamurthi
  • Report: "Towards an Enlightened and Humane Society"
  • Appointed: 7 May 1990; Report tabled: 9 January 1991
  • Common school system – transform govt schools into genuine neighbourhood schools
  • Remove disparities (rural & tribal)
  • Promotion of women education
  • Value education as a continuous process
  • Work Experience/SUPW integrally linked with subjects (content & pedagogy)
  • Work benches & practical schools for vocational ed
  • ECCE – Right to Education for inclusion in Fundamental Rights
  • Navodaya Vidyalayas – three alternatives proposed
✅ MPSC 2014: Work experience should be → (a) Integrally linked with subjects in content and pedagogy
✅ MPSC 2021: Ramamurti Commission was for → (b) Review of NPE 1986
Janardhana Reddy Committee / CABE Committee (1992)
CABEAppointed 31 July 1991
  • To review implementation of NPE parameters; consider Ramamurti report
  • Common school system – implement NPE provisions
  • Navodaya Vidyalaya scheme to continue; one in each district
  • ECCE: Article 45 scope need not be enlarged; Anganwadi workers to play expanded role
  • Operation Blackboard to continue in expanded form (7th Five Year Plan)
  • Dual track approach – UEE + adult literacy simultaneously
  • Vocational stream at +2 to be strengthened; vocational courses from Class IX also
  • District Board of Education to be set up and operationalized
  • Need-based financing for UEE, Adult Literacy, vocationalisation

National Policies on Education in India

NPE 1968 → NPE 1986 → Programme of Action 1992 → National Policy for ICT 2012

NPE 1968NPE 1986PoA 1992ICT Policy 2012NEP 2020
First NPE: Based on Kothari Commission (1964-66) recommendations → rolled out in 1968. ✅ MPSC 2014 Ans: (c) 1968
📗 National Policy on Education – 1968 (NPE 1968)▼
  • Free and Compulsory Education for all children up to age 14 years
  • Status of Teachers: Improve service conditions; academic freedom to publish research
  • Three Language Formula:
    — Hindi-speaking states: Modern Indian language (preferably southern) + Hindi + English
    — Non-Hindi states: Hindi + Regional language + English
  • Promotion of Hindi as link language; facilities for Sanskrit
  • Equalization of Educational Opportunity: Merit-based admission; protect socially deprived
  • Science Education & Research as high priority
  • At least one agricultural university per state
  • Criticized frequent changes and high prices of books
  • Educational Structure: Uniform 10+2+3 pattern across India
  • Increase education investment to 6% of national income
📙 National Policy on Education – 1986 (NPE 1986)▼
  • National System of Education: Common educational structure 10+2+3
  • Operation Blackboard: 3 all-weather rooms, blackboards, maps, charts, toys, library + at least 3 teachers (50% women)
  • Open primary schools in tribal, SC, backward areas on priority
  • Special schools with hostel for severely handicapped at district HQ
  • Vocational education courses at +2 stage
  • IGNOU responsible for coordinating distance learning system
  • Delink degrees from jobs; National Evaluation Organization for voluntary tests
  • Establish rural universities based on Gandhian basic education
📒 Programme of Action – 1992▼
  • Post Ramamurthi & Janardhana Reddy reports
  • Continued Operation Blackboard in expanded form
  • Vocational courses from Class IX
  • District Board of Education to be operationalized
MSBTE – Maharashtra State Board of Technical Education
  • Autonomous board of education in Maharashtra
  • Designs & implements diploma, post diploma & advanced diploma programs
  • Established: 1963; Autonomous status: 1999
  • Headquarters: Mumbai, Maharashtra
  • All polytechnic institutes in Maharashtra affiliated to MSBTE (Govt + Private)
PSSCIVE – Bhopal
  • Pandit Sundarlal Sharma Central Institute of Vocational Education
  • Located at: Bhopal
  • Develops teacher training modules and teacher handbooks
  • Works with universities' Departments of Education & State Governments
✅ DVET 2016: PSSCIVE located at → (d) Bhopal

  • NETF – National Educational Technology Forum
  • Public investment in education → 6% of GDP

🛠️ Vocational Education in NEP 2020
School Level
  • Vocational education from Grade 6 onwards
  • Expanded via Samagra Shiksha Abhiyan
  • 10-day bagless period in Grades 6-8: intern with local vocational experts (carpenters, gardeners, potters, artists)
  • Pre-vocational education for Grades VI to VIII (NCERT framework; MoE guidelines)
  • Skill Based Aptitude Test (SBT) for career guidance
  • NCERT to develop National Curriculum Framework (NCF)
  • Skill labs: hub and spoke model
  • NPST (National Professional Standards for Teachers) by 2022
  • Vocational Teacher Training by NCERT, NIOS, CBSE, State Boards
Higher Education
  • Vocational ed integrated in all HEIs over next decade
  • By 2025: at least 50% of learners to have vocational exposure
  • Incubation centres in HEIs with industry partnerships
  • Short-term certificate courses including soft skills
  • B.Voc. degrees (introduced 2013) to continue
  • NCIVE – National Committee for Integration of Vocational Education
  • GEC – General Education Council (frames graduate attributes)
  • NHEQF in sync with NSQF → ease integration
  • Professional councils as PSSBs (ICAR, VCI, NCTE, CoA, NCVET etc.)
  • NIOS for open vocational courses (ODL system)
📌 NPST – National Professional Standards for Teachers
  • Public statement defining teacher quality + elements of high-quality 21st-century teaching
  • Prepared by PSSB (Professional Standard Setting Body) under GEC
  • Goal: prepare, develop and grow teachers through various career stages
  • Provides clear understanding of attributes, knowledge, skills, ethics all teachers should follow

ECCE – Early Childhood Care Education
  • Integral component of pre-primary education
  • NPE 1986: govt to take care of early childhood education; grants for centres
  • Article 45 (amended Constitution Act 2002): "State shall endeavour to provide ECCE for all children until age of 6 years"
  • RTE Act came into force: 1 April 2010
  • Free pre-school education for children above 3 years

Balwadi
  • Native Marathi name for pre-schools in Maharashtra
  • Developed by Tarabai Modak
  • First Balwadi: Bordi village, Thane district, Maharashtra
  • Started by Nutan Bal Shikshan Sangh in 1945
  • Anganwadi = Angan Balwadi — in neighbourhood of targeted children
Montessori Education
  • Involves children's natural interests & activities (not formal teaching)
  • Emphasis on hands-on learning & real-world skills
  • Started by Italian physician Maria Montessori (1870-1952)
  • Child-centered approach; unique interests & abilities of each student
1. Respect for the Child2. Sensitive Periods for Learning3. A Prepared Environment4. Independence & Discovery
Components: Multi-Age Groupings • Student Choice of Activity • Uninterrupted Blocks of Time
Advantages: Self-motivated learners • Problem-solving skills • Abstract math understanding • Compassion, self-esteem
Balvatika (KVS + NEP 2020)
  • Introduced by Kendriya Vidyalaya Sangathan (KVS) under NEP 2020
  • For children aged 3 to 6 years
  • Emphasizes play-based learning
  • Fosters cognitive, social, emotional & physical development
  • Beyond traditional classroom settings

Universalization of Education (UEE)

UEE Components • School Dropouts • Education For All • Dakar Goals • UDHR Article 26

Universalization of Elementary Education (UEE)
  • Making education up to Class I-VIII available to all children aged 6-14
  • 6-11 = Universalization of Primary Education
  • 6-14 = Universalization of Elementary Education
  • Article 45 (DPSP) + Article 21A (Fundamental Right) → UEE
  • NPE 1968, 1986, PoA 1992 all reiterated UEE commitment
4 Components of UEE:
AccessEnrolmentRetentionAttainment
✅ MPSC 2022: Which is NOT a major component of UEE → (d) Universal Supervision
Causes of School Dropouts in India
  • Child Labour: forced to work in agriculture, manufacturing, domestic service
  • Child Marriages: India has highest number — 34% girls married before 18 (UNICEF 2023)
  • Lack of Interest: studies not found relevant
  • Gender Disparities: safety concerns, household responsibilities, societal norms
  • Health Issues: malnutrition and chronic illnesses
  • Lack of Parental Involvement: lack of awareness or economic pressures
Strategies to Achieve UEE
  • Overcome school dropouts; emphasize retention & attainment over mere enrolment
  • Strengthen non-formal education for working children, girls, disadvantaged groups
  • Decentralization of education
  • Shift focus from educationally backward states to backward districts
Education For All (EFA) – UNESCO
  • Movement started by UNESCO
  • First World Conference: 1990 at Jomtien, Thailand
  • Dakar Conference (World Education Forum): April 2000 — reviewed EFA progress
6 Dakar Goals:
  1. Expand & improve ECCE especially for vulnerable children
  2. All children access free & compulsory primary education by 2015
  3. Learning needs of youth & adults met via equitable life skills access
  4. 50% improvement in adult literacy by 2015, esp. women
  5. Eliminate gender disparities in primary & secondary by 2005; gender equality by 2015
  6. Improve quality → measurable outcomes in literacy, numeracy, life skills
📜 Universal Declaration of Human Rights – Article 26
  • Everyone has the right to education. Elementary stage shall be free
  • Technical & professional education generally available; higher education on merit
  • Education directed to full development of human personality + respect for human rights
  • Parents have prior right to choose kind of education for their children
Note: UDHR approved by UN General Assembly on 10 December 1948. Completed 75 years in 2023. 10th Dec = Human Rights Day

Quality Improvement in Indian Education

7 Strategies • Atal Tinkering Labs • Manodarpan Initiative

1. Teacher Training & Support
  • Regular training: modern methodologies, digital tools, classroom management
  • Improve salaries/incentives especially in rural areas
2. Emphasis on ECE
  • Expanding quality ECE bridges early learning gaps
  • Active involvement of parents is important
3. Infrastructure & Digital Access
  • Basic facilities: clean toilets, libraries, adequate classrooms
  • Digital devices & internet in rural areas → bridge digital divide
4. Assessment Reforms
  • Holistic model: participation, projects, practical skills
  • Evaluate understanding & application (not rote)
5. Research & Innovation
  • More research opportunities at school & university levels
  • Collaborate with international universities
6. Education Policy & Governance
  • Grant local authorities more power; tailor to specific needs
  • Decentralization especially in diverse states
Atal Tinkering Labs (ATL)
  • Established by Atal Innovation Mission (initiative of NITI Aayog)
  • Foster curiosity, creativity, imagination in young minds
  • Inculcate: design mindset, computational thinking, adaptive learning, physical computing
  • Hands-on DIY workspace; learn innovation skills
  • Children work with tools to understand STEM
7. Mental Health & Well-being
  • Mental health services and academic counselling
  • Move away from high-stakes competitive exams; focus on holistic development
Manodarpan Initiative (Ministry of Education)
  • Wide range of psychosocial support activities for students, teachers & families
  • Focus: Mental Health & Emotional Wellbeing during COVID and beyond
  • National Toll-free Helpline: 8448440632

  • Tele-counselling for mental health

Impact of Globalization & Privatization on Education

Positive & Negative impacts • Commercialization • Digital Transformation

Globalization: "The intensification of world-wide social relations which link distant localities in such a way that local happenings are shaped by events occurring many miles away and vice-versa."
✅ Positive Impacts
  • Access to Global Knowledge: Indian education opened to international standards; English proficiency, digital literacy, cross-cultural skills
  • Rise of Private Institutions: Higher quality facilities and resources to meet youth demand
  • Improved Quality: Competitive environment → STEM education, soft skills, technical training incorporated
  • Vocational & Skill-Based Education: Revealed need to align workforce with international standards; surge in internship partnerships
  • Digital Transformation: Online courses, digital resources; learning more accessible
❌ Negative Impacts
  • Commercialization: Institutions prioritize profit → rote learning, lack of critical thinking; "education for profit" vs "education for all"
  • Public-Private Disparity: Private institutions costly; accessible mainly to affluent families
  • Impact on Public Education: Privatization diverts funding from public schools; quality gap reinforces inequality
  • Widening Educational Divide: Low-income families excluded from quality private education; socioeconomic gap widens
